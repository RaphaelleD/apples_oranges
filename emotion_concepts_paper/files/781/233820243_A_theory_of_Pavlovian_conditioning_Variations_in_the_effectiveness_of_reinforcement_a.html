Sodium amytal and partially reinforced runway performance
Article
Full-text available
  • June 1963
  • Journal of Experimental Psychology
  • Allan R Wagner
Acquisition performance of rats in a simple runway was investigated as a function of percentage reinforced trials (100% vs. 50%) and injection solution received prior to daily training (sodium amytal vs. isotonic saline) in a 2 X 2 factorial design. The results obtained from 4 groups of 12 Ss showed 50% reinforcement to lead to faster final speeds than 100% reinforcement with saline control injections; whereas this order was reversed under training with sodium amytal. The results were predicted from a frustration theory interpretation of random partial reinforcement and the assumption that anticipatory frustration is particularly suspectible to the depressant action of sodium amytal.

Distribution-of-Trials Effects in Pavlovian Conditioning. An Apparent Involvement of Inhibitory Backward Conditioning With Short Intertrial Intervals
Article
Full-text available
  • October 1985
  • Journal of Experimental Psychology Animal Behavior Processes
  • Michael F. Ewing
  • Mark B. Larew
  • Allan R Wagner
Two experiments, using a conditioned emotional response (CER) procedure, investigated the associative consequences of an isolated feature of massed versus spaced schedules of Pavlovian conditioning. Ss were 24 male albino rats. In each experiment, comparisons were arranged between 2 conditioned stimulus/stimuli (CS) that were separately trained in an intermixed daily schedule such that 1 CS was consistently separated from adjacent, preceding CS/unconditioned stimulus/stimuli (UCS) pairings by a relatively short (1-min) interval and the other by a relatively long (10-min) interval. In Exp I, the comparison CS were reinforced during the experimental phase. In Exp II, the comparison CS were pretrained to be excitatory stimuli and were nonreinforced during the experimental phase. When responding to the CS was assessed in separate nonreinforced test sessions, in which all surrounding intertrial intervals were matched, the CS trained closer to the preceding reinforced trial evidenced less acquisition (Exp I) and more extinction (Exp II). Results are not predictable from J. Gibbon and P. D. Balsam's (1981) description of spreading association but suggest an involvement of inhibitory backward conditioning with short intertrial intervals as anticipated by A. R. Wagner (1981). The role of reinforcement and nonreinforcement in an "apparent frustration effect."
Article
Full-text available
  • March 1959
  • Journal of Experimental Psychology
  • Allan R Wagner
Two interpretations have been proposed to account for the finding that responses immediately following the nonreinforcement of a previously reinforced response are more vigorous than responses immediately following reinforcement. The frustration hypothesis proposes that this difference in response vigor, FE, is due to an increment in drive level (D) resulting from nonreinforcement, whereas the response-depression hypothesis proposes that it may be attributed to a decrement in D as a result of reinforcement." Data support the frustration hypothesis and contradict predictions from the response-depression hypothesis.

Modulation of a Discrete Pavlovian Conditioned Reflex by a Putative Emotive Pavlovian Conditioned Stimulus
Article
Full-text available
  • August 1991
  • Journal of Experimental Psychology Animal Behavior Processes
  • Sussan Brandon
  • Allan R Wagner
Three experiments showed the modulation of a rabbit eyeblink conditioned response (CR) to a Pavlovian conditioned stimulus (CS) by 30-s stimuli (A & B) that had been differentially paired with paraorbital shock. The CS (Y) was a 1,050-ms cue that had been paired with paraorbital shock outside A or B. In testing, the amplitude of CRs was greater when Y was presented within A than within B. Differential modulation occurred whether shock in A had been preceded by another 1,050-ms cue, X(AX+,BX-;Experiment 1) or not (A+B-;Experiment 2). Experiment 3 compared the technique of Experiment 1 (AX+) with that of Experiment 2 (A+) and found the latter to be advantageous for facilitation of CRs to Y by A. These data are consistent with the predictions of a model of Pavlovian conditioning (AESOP, Wagner & Brandon, 1989) that distinguishes between emotive and sensory conditioning as did Konorski (1967).

Short-term memory for "surprising" versus "expected" unconditioned stimuli in Pavlovian conditioning
Article
Full-text available
  • May 1975
  • Journal of Experimental Psychology Animal Behavior Processes
  • William S. Terry
  • Allan R Wagner
Rabbits were trained via a Pavlovian eyelid-conditioning regime to respond differentially to a conditioned stimulus (CSR) depending on whether or not that stimulus was preceded several seconds earlier by a preparatory stimulus consisting of an electric shock otherwise employed as an unconditioned stimulus (UCS). Following training, differential responding to CSR varied systematically with the interval between the preparatory stimulus and CSR, consistent with assumptions concerning decay of information in short-term memory. The major finding, however, involved the test effects of announcing the preparatory stimulus by either a conditioned stimulus with which it had otherwise been paired (CS+) or a conditioned stimulus with which it had otherwise been systematically unpaired (CS-). The greater differential responding to the following CSR in the latter case is taken to support the notion that a "surprising" UCS is more likely to be retained (rehearsed) in short-term memory than is an "expected" UCS.
