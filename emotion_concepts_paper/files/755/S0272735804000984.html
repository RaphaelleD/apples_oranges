Attention processes in the maintenance and treatment of social phobia: hypervigilance, avoidance and self-focused attention

Abstract

Four domains of evidence regarding the relationship between attention and social phobia are reviewed: (1) possible maintaining factor, (2) causal relationship, (3) specific relationship and (4) mediator of change during treatment. Two areas of research are covered and integrated: vigilance-avoidance of social threat stimuli and self-focused attention. There is empirical support for these processes as possible maintaining factors that are specific to patients and non-clinical samples with high levels of social anxiety. There is reasonable evidence to promote the use of attentional strategies in the treatment of social phobia, although better controlled studies are required. We propose six overlapping mechanisms that could be responsible for change: reduced vigilance; reduced avoidance; reduced self-focused attention; mindfulness; increased attentional control; increased self-esteem.

1. Introduction

Attention processes are assumed to play an important role in social anxiety and social phobia. In this article we will review existing evidence for a maintaining and causal role of these processes in social fears, review evidence for the effectiveness of attention training in social phobia, and discuss possible mechanisms that can explain the effectiveness of attention procedures in treating social phobia.

We have classified the studies according to two important theoretical approaches to attention in social phobia that will be described in more detail later; (I) hypervigilance and (II) self-focused attention. Within the area of anxiety, hypervigilance is typically used to refer to the selective attention for threat-related stimuli rather than neutral stimuli (e.g. Eysenck, 1992, Mogg & Bradley, 1998). It has been suggested that hypervigilance is soon followed by avoidance: a defense reaction to prevent or minimise danger (e.g. Mogg, Bradley, de Bono, & Painter, 1997). Self-focused attention refers to the bi-directionality of human social consciousness (Duval & Wicklund, 1972), which is focused on the self or the external environment. Self-focused attention in a social context can result in a state of objective self-awareness, a state in which the person takes himself to be an object.

We discuss the conducted experiments in these two areas relevant for our understanding of the role of attention processes in social anxiety and social phobia. The following questions will be addressed:

(I)

What is the evidence for and against a possible maintaining role of attention processes in social anxiety?

(II)

What is the evidence for and against a causal role of attention processes in social anxiety (or, are attention processes just a by-product of social anxiety)?

(III)

Which attention processes are specific for social anxiety and not related to other types of anxiety and psychopathology?

(IV)

How are attention processes related to change in social anxiety?

2. Hypervigilance and avoidance

2.1. Introduction

Several models have proposed that anxious individuals are hypervigilant to threat (Beck et al., 1985, Eysenck, 1992, Eysenck, 1997, Mogg & Bradley, 1998, Mogg et al., 1997, Williams et al., 1988, Williams et al., 1997). Each of these accounts also proposes that attention is directed to stimuli that are relevant to the concerns of the specific anxiety disorders (specificity hypothesis). Generally, experimental evidence across a range of anxiety disorders supports this proposal (for reviews, see Mogg & Bradley, 1998, Harvey et al., 2004, Williams et al., 1997). Certain authors have described the process of hypervigilance in more detail. For example, Eysenck (1992) proposes a broadening of attention during environmental scanning, followed by a narrowing of attention (fixation) when a stimulus is being processed. However, there is insufficient evidence at this stage to make strong conclusions about these features of hypervigilance in the anxiety disorders.

The vigilance-avoidance hypothesis (e.g. Mogg et al., 1997) suggests that hypervigilance is followed by avoidance of the threat stimulus, which serves a defensive function. Within social situations, hypervigilance-avoidance may result in the initial enhanced processing of threatening stimuli (e.g. a frowning face), yet the reduced opportunity to habituate to, or reappraise the stimulus as non-threatening (e.g. appraise the frown as showing concentration rather than criticism). Therefore, the threat stimulus maintains its capacity to elicit anxiety and to be interpreted as threatening.

Several models of social anxiety and social phobia include elements of the vigilance-avoidance hypothesis (Beck et al., 1985, Clark & Wells, 1995, Rapee & Heimberg, 1997). They each state that attention in social anxiety is directed towards threatening stimuli that relate specifically to concerns about negative evaluation. For example, Clark and Wells (1995) emphasise that when people with social phobia fear that they will be negatively evaluated they shift their attention towards internal threat stimuli such as their own anxiety response. This interferes with their opportunity to reappraise the situation as less threatening, conflicts with their social performance, and leads them to conclude that they may look as anxious as they feel inside. Clark and Wells (1995) also propose that people with social phobia may also avoid attending to the social situation as a form of safety behaviour, for example reducing eye contact so as not to be brought into the conversation and end up making a fool of themselves. Again, this prevents effective processing of the social situation. Rapee and Heimberg (1997) also state that attention can be directed to threatening internal stimuli and that safety behaviours can entail avoidance of social interaction. In addition, they emphasise that individuals with social phobia scan their environment for any signs of impending negative evaluation, will detect such signs rapidly, and will have difficulty disengaging attention from them.

Several experimental paradigms have been used to test the hypothesis that social phobia is associated with hypervigilance and/or avoidance with respect to social threat stimuli. Four classes of paradigm will be described here: the emotional Stroop test (Mathews & MacLeod, 1985; Stroop, 1935), the modified dot-probe task (MacLeod, Mathews & Tata, 1986), the visual search task, and the use of an eye tracking apparatus. 2.2. The emotional Stroop test

The most commonly used paradigm is the emotional Stroop test. This paradigm has become very familiar to researchers of cognitive processes across a range of psychological disorders. In the emotional Stroop test, the participant must name the colour of words printed in different coloured inks. Selective attention to threat is thought to be indexed by a longer time to name the ink colour of threat words relative to neutral words. However, it is not clear that this effect is actually a result of selective attention to the word, because the longer time taken to colour-name could be caused by other factors. First, the threatening words may create an emotional reaction, such as a startle, that inhibits any response and leads to longer reaction times (Cloitre, Heimberg, Holt, & Liebowitz, 1992). Second, the emotional Stroop effect may be capturing ‘cognitive avoidance’, in that the longer colour-naming latencies may simply reflect attempts by the individual to suppress the threatening meaning of the word (Ruiter & Brosschott, 1994). A third possibility is that the emotional Stroop effect is a result of mental preoccupation on themes related to the emotional words, which produces longer reaction times (Wells & Matthews, 1994). Thus, although published studies often suggest that the emotional Stroop interference effect reflects hypervigilance, it may be the product of a range of cognitive processes in addition to hypervigilance. Therefore, results from the emotional Stroop task should be interpreted with some caution.

Despite the ambiguity of the findings of the emotional Stroop test, it has been used extensively in social phobia. In support of the content specificity hypothesis, several studies have found that people with social phobia are slower to colour-name social threat words but not physical threat words (Becker et al., 2001, Hope et al., 1990, Lundh & Öst, 1996, Maidenberg et al., 1996, Mattia et al., 1993). However, one study showed no clear distinction between Stroop interference for physical and social threat words (Amir et al., 1996) and a study of children with social phobia showed no evidence of increased Stroop interference for either class of threatening words (Kindt, Bögels, & Morren, 2003).

Several studies have focused more specifically on the kinds of words that can create emotional Stroop interference. Spector, Pecknold and Libman (2003) found that, compared with nonanxious controls, patients with generalised social phobia were slower to name the colours of social threat words describing negative evaluation (e.g. criticize) and words denoting the observable aspects of anxiety (e.g. blushing), but not anxiety words that were less noticeable to others (e.g. palpitations). These findings support the view that it is other people's negative evaluations that are key to social phobia. In a further study of specificity, McNeil et al. (1995) explored the effects of subtype of social phobia on the emotional Stroop. Consistent with the content specificity hypothesis, they found that only patients with generalised social phobia, but not those with specific speech-related social phobia, showed greater interference for words relating to generalized social fears (e.g. party, conversation). Both groups of patients, on the other hand, showed greater Stroop interference for words relating to speeches and relating to negative evaluation. Further Stroop studies have compared patients with social phobia with other patient groups to test whether the increased Stroop interference effect for social threat words is specific to social phobia. The evidence is conflicting with one study showing that patients with panic disorder show no attentional bias for social threat words relative to social phobia (Hope et al., 1990), one finding a non-significant trend for a difference (Van Niekerk, Moeller, & Nortje, 1999) and others finding attention towards social threat words in panic disorder (Maidenberg et al., 1996) and generalized anxiety disorder (Becker et al., 2001).

Further studies have explored the consequences of the experimental context on the Stroop effect. Amir et al. (1996) found that the emotional Stroop effect for social threat words was reduced when patients with social phobia were expecting to give a speech. They concluded that the patients with social phobia were more able to use strategic effort to suppress the emotional Stroop effect when under real socially threatening conditions. If this strategy were to be interpreted as a way to avoid the social threat words, one may conclude that people with social phobia switch to avoid social threat when in a threatening situation that primes their fears. Lundh and Öst (1996) used a mirror in order to try to increase self-focus during testing, but found no differential effect of this manipulation on emotional Stroop interference for social threat words.

In summary, patients with social phobia typically take longer to colour-name threatening words that relate to their specific concerns about social evaluation, but not typically to words that are unrelated to these concerns. However, other groups of patients may also show this effect for social threat words and so the effect may not be specific to social phobia. Most importantly, the findings using the Stroop test may not reflect selective attention and are likely to involve other significant cognitive processes such as mental preoccupation.

2.3. The modified dot-probe task using words

The modified dot-probe paradigm circumvents many of the problems of the emotional Stroop test. In this task, the participant first focuses their eyes on a central fixation cross. After around 1000 ms, the cross is replaced by two stimuli on a computer screen, one above the other. For example, the screen may display a threatening word, such as ‘ridiculed’, and a neutral word, such as ‘navigated’. The words disappear after a brief period, usually less than half a second, and a dot appears on the screen, either in the place where the threatening word had been displayed, or in the place where the neutral word had been displayed. The aim of the task is to press a button as soon as the dot is detected. The modified dot-probe task has several advantages over the emotional Stroop test. First, because one example of each of two classes of stimuli (e.g. threatening and neutral) are presented on the screen simultaneously, it can be said to truly measure selective attention towards one particular class of stimuli. Second, attentional bias is indexed by a faster response to the probe, and therefore any other factors, such as mental preoccupation, that could slow down response time, can be discounted as an explanation for the effect. Third, the paradigm can assess both selective attention towards a threat stimulus (hypervigilance), and selective attention away from a threat stimulus (avoidance).

Interestingly, despite the promising design of the modified dot-probe task, several studies have failed to find clear evidence for selective attention to social threat words in social phobia (Amir et al., 2003, Asmundson & Stein, 1994, Horenstein & Segui, 1997) and social anxiety (Mansell, Ehlers, Clark, & Chen, 2002). Asmundson and Stein (1994) found that patients with social phobia were faster to respond to the probe in either position when they had read the social threat word out loud. This could be interpreted as a general hypervigilance primed by social threat, but not an attentional bias towards social threat stimuli. Amir et al. (2003) found an attentional bias to social threat words in social phobia, but their finding was also qualified. They found that the patients took longer to respond to the probe when a social threat word occurred in the opposite spatial location, but they were no faster to detect the probe when it had occurred in the same location as the social threat word. This led the authors to conclude that social phobia may be associated with a reduced ability to disengage from social threat cues (as suggested by Rapee & Heimberg, 1997), rather than hypervigilance towards them. Future modified dot-probe studies may have to control for this possibility. Horenstein and Segui (1997) found no attentional bias to threat in patients with social phobia relative to controls.

None of the above studies assessed attentional bias in a relevant social context, such as a social interaction. To address this omission, Mansell et al. (2002) tested whether high and low socially anxious individuals attended to social threat words and positive social words, with and without the presence of social evaluation (the threat of a speech). There was no effect of social anxiety, but trait general anxiety was associated with attention towards negative relative to positive social words. The threat of a speech increased attention to positive and negative social words, but this effect did not interact with social anxiety.

Taken together, the above findings appear to call into the question the view that social anxiety is associated with hypervigilance to social threat words, as suggested by the findings of the emotional Stroop test. However, a recent study using the modified dot-probe suggests that comorbid depression and anxiety may be obscurring hypervigilance for social threat words in social phobia. Musa, Lepine, Clark, Mansell, and Ehlers (2003) found hypervigilance to social threat words only when those with depression were excluded; patients with depression showed avoidance of the social threat words like the control group. They also noted that the hypervigilance was specific to social threat words and not physical threat words only in those patients without other comorbid anxiety disorders, namely generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder or obsessive-compulsive disorder. Thus, it may be possible to identify hypervigilance to social threat words in social phobia, but only with a relatively pure sample. Consistent with this view, Vassilopoulos (in press) found that high socially anxious individuals attended towards all emotional words (including positive, social threat and physical threat) when they were presented for 250 ms. Interestingly, this study found that high socially anxious individuals avoided the emotional words when presented for 500 ms, indicating support for the vigilance-avoidance hypothesis, albeit not specific to social threat.

Ononaiye, Turpin and Reidy (2002) have also found an attentional bias to social threat words using the dot-probe task. They compared attention to words relating to negative social evaluation with words relating to anxiety symptoms, under conditions of social threat (a speech) or no threat. Interestingly, they found that high socially anxious individuals attended to social evaluative words in the no-threat condition, but towards somatic words (anxiety symptoms) in the speech condition; each of these effects remained when accounting for general trait anxiety and depression. This finding supports the view that attention may be directed to internal threat cues when a real-life social threat is present (Clark & Wells, 1995), and implicating the importance of self-focused attention which is covered in more detail later.

Overall, the modified dot-probe task using words finds only tentative support for the vigilance-avoidance hypothesis. Many factors obscure any clear interpretation of the findings and involve problems with disengagement from threat stimuli, the presence of comorbidity, the timing of presentation, the nature of the social threat words and the presence of a real social threat.

2.4. The modified dot-probe task using faces

Besides providing ambiguous findings, measuring attention to visually presented words may have limited ecological validity. In real social situations, the social threat stimuli are other peoples' responses, facial expressions and verbal comments. Therefore, several studies have explored selective attention to images of facial expressions using the modified dot-probe task. Bradley et al. (1997) initially found no relationship between a measure of social anxiety, the Fear of Negative Evaluation scale (Watson & Friend, 1969) and attention to threatening versus neutral facial expressions. However, in a later study this group of researchers found that high socially anxious individuals, as measured by the Social Avoidance and Distress scale (Watson & Friend, 1969) selectively attended to briefly (17 ms) presented masked threatening faces (Mogg & Bradley, 2002). This effect remained when covarying for levels of trait general anxiety and depression. Mogg, Philippot, and Bradley (2004) presented patients with social phobia and normal controls with happy-neutral and angry-neutral face pairs at display times of 500 and 1250 ms. They found that the patients selectively attended to the angry faces at 500 ms in contrast to the controls. There was a non-significant tendency for this effect to reverse at 1250 ms, with levels of social anxiety correlating negatively rather than positively with attention to angry faces. Thus, when assessing attentional bias using images of facial expressions in the modified dot-probe task, social anxiety can be associated with hypervigilance to social threat. It should however be noted that hypervigilance to threatening faces is not specific to social phobia, but also occurs in GAD (e.g. Bradley, Mogg, White, Groom, & de Bono, 1999), and further that the effect may reverse when longer display times are used; this effect may represent a stronger predomination of attentional avoidance rather than vigilance that develops over a short space of time.

Further studies have found avoidance of faces using the modified dot-probe task. Initially, in an unpublished study, high socially anxious individuals were found to attend away from negative faces when they were presented alongside neutral faces for 1000 ms (Yuen, 1994). Mansell, Clark, Ehlers and Chen (1999) developed a version of the dot-probe task that attempted to mimick the two contrasting kinds of stimuli to which a socially anxious person may attend in a real-life situation: a negative, neutral or positive face next to an everyday object (e.g. a chair) for 500 ms. High and low socially anxious participants carried out the task in one of two situations: social-evaluative threat (expecting to give a speech) or no social-evaluative threat. They found that the high socially anxious individuals who were expecting to give a speech attended away from the negative and positive facial expressions, relative to the low socially anxious individuals. There were no effects of social anxiety in the no-threat condition, although general trait anxiety was associated with vigilance to negative faces. The same paradigm was administered to people with social phobia and non-clinical controls (Chen, Ehlers, Clark, & Mansell, 2002) and the patients were found to avoid the facial expressions relative to the control group, without the presence of an explicit social threat condition. One study has explored attention to faces in children (Stirling, Eley, & Clark, submitted for publication). They presented pairs of negative-neutral, negative-positive and positive-neutral faces for 1000 ms and found that social anxiety was associated with avoidance of negative faces. Finally, a further modified paradigm compared attention to external cues (which were mostly images of faces) with attention to internal cues (a vibration of the finger thought to indicate changes in the participants' levels of arousal). This study found that high speech anxious individuals directed their attention away from the external threat and towards the internal cue, but only within the context of expecting to give a speech (Mansell, Clark, & Ehlers, 2003). Again, no effects were found in the no-threat condition.

In summary, modified dot-probe studies using facial expressions as stimuli are more promising than word versions and have separately shown both vigilance and avoidance. The studies that have found vigilance tend to display the images for brief periods, or display two faces simultaneously, or tend not to have used a real social threat such as a speech. Studies that have found avoidance of faces tend to display the stimuli for longer, or tend to display a face opposite a non-social cue such as an object, or tend to use a real-life social threat. These findings tentatively support the vigilance-avoidance hypothesis. Vigilance may predominate when it is ambiguous as to whether a real social threat is present, and it may occur relatively quickly. Avoidance, on the other hand, may predominate as a defensive mechanism when the individual already feels negative evaluated, when there is a neutral non-social stimulus available on which to focus attention, and it may take longer to develop. The findings are consistent with one unpublished study (Vassilopoulos, 2002) which found vigilance to faces followed by avoidance of faces in high socially anxious individuals using a modified dot-probe task similar to that used by Mansell et al. (1999) and involving conditions of social threat.

2.5. Detection paradigms

Detection paradigms are a more direct way of assessing hypervigilance because they directly assess detection of threatening stimuli; a common example is the visual search task. In this design, a target stimulus is presented among an array of background stimuli. Selective attention to threat is indexed by a shorter time to detect the target stimulus when it is threatening relative to when it is neutral. The visual search task can also be used to assess distraction by threatening stimuli, which is a further index of selective attention; the bias is indexed by a slower response time to the target stimulus when the background stimuli are threatening compared to when they are neutral. Rinck, Becker, Kellermann and Roth (2003) presented patients with specific (public-speaking related) social phobia, patients with generalized anxiety disorders, and non-clinical controls, with a visual search task involving words that were concern-related, neutral or positive. They found that patients with generalized anxiety were more easily distracted by background stimuli that were generalized anxiety-related words, but the patients with social phobia showed no distraction by speech-related words, nor an enhanced detection of speech-related words. Thus, they found no evidence of attention to social threat words in social phobia as assessed by this paradigm.

Several further studies have used ecologically valid cues within detection paradigms. Three of these studies found evidence of enhanced detection of negative social cues in social phobia (Gilboa-Schechtman, Foa, & Amir, 1999) and social anxiety (Veljaca & Rapee, 1998, Perowne & Mansell, 2002), whereas three of these studies failed to find any evidence in social anxiety (Esteves, 1999, Pozo et al., 1991, Winton et al., 1995).

In the visual search study by Gilboa-Schechtman et al. (1999), participants were presented with a screen of 12 photographic images of facial expressions. On critical trials, a target face was displayed among an array of background faces of a different emotion (e.g. an angry face in a happy crowd, or a happy face in an angry crowd). Compared to nonanxious controls, patients with social phobia showed faster detection times for angry faces than happy faces in a neutral crowd. Esteves (1999) used a similar design to Gilboa-Schechtman et al. (1999), but presented schematic facial expressions (rather than photographic images of actual faces) to high and low socially anxious participants. In one study the participants were currently being evaluated by a video camera, and in a second study they were not being socially evaluated. In both studies, Esteves (1999) found that all participants were faster at detecting angry faces, but there was no difference in latency between high and low socially anxious participants. Veljaca and Rapee (1998) used a more naturalistic design than Gilboa-Schechtman et al. (1999) and Esteves (1999). High and low socially anxious participants gave a speech to three audience members who had been trained to elicit nonverbal signs of negative and positive evaluation at irregular intervals. During the speech, the participants pressed one of two buttons to indicate whether they had noticed either a negative or positive behaviour. Using a signal detection analysis, Veljaca and Rapee (1998) found that the high socially anxious participants were more accurate at detecting negative behaviours, while low socially anxious participants were more accurate at detecting positive behaviours. This study demonstrates a novel design with a high ecological validity, but there would appear to be uncertainties about how well it could have been controlled. The audience members were asked to display a behaviour “when they felt it was most appropriate”, and thus the behaviours could have been timed to coincide with specific behaviours from the participants themselves, leading to a contamination by the participants' own behaviour. An effective method of bypassing this form of contamination was developed in an earlier study by Pozo et al. (1991). In this study, high and low socially anxious participants answered a series of questions about themselves, replying to what they thought was a closed circuit TV connection to another person, but was in fact a video tape of an individual portraying fixed proportions of negative, neutral and positive facial expressions. Thus, the interaction was perceived to be real, but was in fact pre-recorded and therefore controlled. Pozo et al. (1991) found that no evidence of enhanced detection of different facial expressions in high socially anxious individuals.

Perowne and Mansell (2002) adapted the video-presentation paradigm used by Pozo et al. (1991) to incorporate the kinds of non-verbal behaviours used by Veljaca and Rapee (1998), thereby circumventing some of the problems with the latter study. High and low socially anxious individuals were asked to make a speech to a screen displaying six people whom they were led to believe were actually watching them live in the next room. Two audience members exhibited positive behaviours, two exhibited neutral behaviours and two exhibited negative behaviours. In contrast to the low social anxiety group, who selectively discriminated the positive audience members, the high social anxiety group selectively discriminated the negative audience members, yet they were no more accurate at discriminating the negative behaviours that the audience members had elicited. The authors concluded that the high socially anxious individuals may have shown an initial attentional bias to the negative audience members, but then subsequently chose to avoid them thereby showing no advantage in discriminating their actual negative behaviours during the course of the speech. Indeed, the high socially anxious individuals reported greater self-focused attention in this study (see later).

In summary, studies using detection paradigms indicate that social anxiety may be associated with hypervigilance for social threat under conditions that more closely mimick real-life situations, such as photographs of faces or real behaviours from audience members. However, there are some indications that the attentional bias may not be sustained over time, possibly owing to avoidance. In most detection paradigms, the tendency to avoid a social threat cue would be blocked by the instructions of the task (to detect the social cue). Therefore only detection tasks that do not explicitly instruct participants to detect the social cue would be expected to find attentional avoidance.

2.6. Eye movement and visual scanning

Finally, maybe the most direct method to assess selective attention is the use of an eye tracking apparatus. This records the exact position of eye gaze over time without requiring the participant to provide an explicit response. This provides a relatively naturalistic assessment of attention. Selective attention is indexed by sudden eye movements (saccades) to a stimulus and also by the duration of time that an individual fixates on the stimulus. Eye movements are known to be partly correlated with selective attention as measured by other methods. However, they are not exactly the same measure, because it is possible to attend to stimuli that are outside the focal point of eye gaze.

While assessing eye movement, Horley, Williams, Gonsalvez and Gordon (2003) presented patients with social phobia and controls with photographs of facial expressions. They found that the patients spent less time than the controls scanning prominent facial features such as the eyes. This finding could be interpreted as an avoidance of signals of social evaluation. Notably, similar findings have also been found in patients with affective disorders and patients with schizophrenia (e.g. Loughland, Williams, & Gordon, 2002).

Garner, Mogg, and Bradley (2003) explored eye movements during the presentation of happy-neutral face pairs in two related studies, angry neutral face pairs and face-object pairs. Under non-threatening conditions, the high socially anxious individuals initially directed their gaze towards neutral faces relative to objects more often than the low socially anxious individuals, whereas both groups oriented and maintained their gaze towards emotional faces. Thus, social anxiety was associated with hypervigilance to neutral faces, but the hypervigilance to positive and negative faces was no greater than in low socially anxious individuals. A second study differed from the first in that it involved the threat of a speech. This time, the high socially anxious individuals showed reduced orienting and maintenance of attention to neutral faces relative to low socially anxious individuals. In addition, they showed quicker orienting toward emotional faces, but they maintained their attention on the emotional faces for a shorter period of time than the low socially anxious individuals. This study shows perhaps the most compelling evidence for both vigilance and avoidance of social threat in social anxiety, and deserves replication. Social anxiety appeared to lead to a general avoidance of neutral facial expressions, and a brief vigilance followed by prolonged avoidance of emotional facial expressions.

2.7. Summary across four different paradigms

The emotional Stroop task is the most widely used, and has provided relatively consistent evidence that social phobia is associated with a specific attentional bias relating to social threat, but processes of vigilance and avoidance cannot easily be a disentangled in this paradigm. In other paradigms, where words have been used as stimuli, the findings provide only tentative support for the vigilance-avoidance hypothesis, whereas the use of images of faces and real social cues has led to more positive findings. There is evidence both for a brief hypervigilance to social threat cues, and a more sustained avoidance of social threat cues during more realistic social-evaluative situations. A minority of studies (Mansell et al., 1999, Garner et al., 2003, Vassilopoulos, 2002, Vassilopoulos, in press), only one of which is published, have shown both vigilance and avoidance within the same paradigm, and further evidence is required to determine the conditions that differentiate between these two opposing but functionally related cognitive processes. There are also indications that during real social threat, attention switches to internal cues (Mansell et al., 2003; Ononaiye et al., 2002; Perowne & Mansell, 2002), and this process is reviewed in detail later.

2.8. What is the evidence of a causal role?

No studies of social anxiety or social phobia have systematically manipulated attentional bias to social threat cues and explored its effects. Some tentative evidence of a causal role of avoidance of social threat comes from studies in which increasing attention to the social environment can lead to reductions in social anxiety (e.g. Wells & Papageorgiou, 1998a, Wells & Papageorgiou, 1998b); however this manipulation is also likely to have the effect of reducing self-focused attention. We will return at the end to the question of whether avoidance of social cues and self-focused attention are related.

2.9. What is the evidence of specificity to social anxiety?

We reviewed evidence that patients with psychological disorders other than social phobia show increased emotional Stroop interference for social threat words. However, levels of social anxiety were not assessed in these patients and they could account for the effect. The same explanation could account for why patients with affective disorders and patients with schizophrenia show less scanning of facial features; social anxiety is common in both of these groups. Future studies need to assess levels of social anxiety in patient control populations. Nevertheless, it is encouraging that certain studies which have identified both hypervigilance for social threat (Mogg & Bradley, 2002; Ononaiye et al., 2002) and avoidance of social threat (Mansell et al., 1999) have found that the effect remains when covarying for general trait anxiety and depression. This provides some indication that the pattern of vigilance and avoidance to social threat cues may be specific to social anxiety.

3. Self-focused attention

3.1. Introduction

During social interactions a person's attention can be focused internally, to the aspects of the self, like his or her own arousal, behaviour, thoughts, emotions, or appearance, or externally, towards the other person(s), the task at hand, or the environment. Self-focused attention refers to the process whereby attention is directed towards internal self-relevant stimuli. The term has also been used to refer to ruminating and dwelling on thoughts about the self (e.g. Teasdale, Segal, & Williams, 1995) although there is some disagreement about whether this use of the term is applicable and should instead be regarded as a thought process rather than an attentional process (Harvey et al., 2004). It has been suggested that a successful social interaction requires an appropriate balance between self-focused and externally focused attention (Wells & Mathews, 1994). Heightened self-focused attention can induce a state of objective self-awareness, “a state in which a person takes him/herself to be an object” (Wicklund, 1975, p. 234) or “a possible detached observer-description” (Taylor, 1985, p. 66). The state of taking an observer perspective can be thought of as a component of self-focused attention that is associated with public self-consciousness (see later).

Heightened self-focused attention is assumed to play an important role in the maintenance of social phobia. Several authors have proposed that socially anxious persons direct too much attention to themselves during social interactions, and have little attention for their task, the other person(s), and their environment (Clark & Wells, 1995, Hartman, 1983, Hartman, 1984, Hope et al., 1989, Rapee & Heimberg, 1997). As a result of their internal focus, they are assumed to engage in self-evaluation and, therefore, become inconveniently aware of their fear and arousal, and of possible flaws in their behaviour. Moreover, lack of outward directed attention might result in actually “unskilled” behaviour.

Since the excellent review of Hope et al. (1989) on attentional focus in social phobia, several studies have been conducted to further investigate the relationship between self-focused attention and social anxiety. After a short overview on the effects of attentional focus on relevant aspects of social behaviour in non-clinical populations, we will discuss correlational as well as causal evidence for a relationship between trait and state attentional focus and social anxiety/social phobia.

3.2. Effects of self-focused attention on non-clinical populations

Results of experimental social psychological research suggest a causal relationship between heightened self-focused attention and increase in negative affect, whereas self-focus on positive aspects or following a positive event lowers negative affect (see the meta-analysis of Mor & Winquist, 2002). In the same way, many experiments have shown that self-focused attention increases self-evaluative thinking and increases the likelihood of self-attributions for the outcome of a situation (e.g., Duval & Wicklund, 1973). Duval and Wicklund's (1972) theory of objective self-awareness states that focussing attention on the self induces self-evaluative thinking, in which one's perceived behaviour and appearance is compared with a standard. Only when the comparison is negative, will a person experience negative affect. The comparison can be negative when a person holds very high standards or when his/her self-perceived behaviour and appearance is negative. As an example of the beneficial effects of self-focused attention on thinking, when a group of non-clinical volunteers were made self-aware, they made more internal attributions of success and external attributions of failure (Chen, Yates, & McGinnies, 1988).

The relationship between self-focused attention and arousal is particularly complex. Although it has been clearly demonstrated that heightened arousal serves to focus attention inward, the reverse relation is less clear. There is evidence to suggest that self-focused attention increases correct awareness of physiological reactions (Scheier, Carver, & Matthews, 1983), and causes an overestimation of physiological reactions in people who suffer from heightened awareness of such reactions. However, Carver and Scheier (1981) found that self-focused attention, as manipulated by a mirror, did not result in increased skin conductance of an unselected sample while copying text, but did so where self-focused attention was manipulated by the presence of an observer. These data show that the presence of another person may produce a non-specific arousal state. In sum, research on unselected samples shows that self-focused attention either leads to a more accurate or to a heightened report of arousal, but there is little evidence yet for a causal relationship between self-focused attention and increase in actual physiological arousal.

There is some evidence to suggest that self-focusing decreases performance at social and non-social tasks, but only under evaluative conditions. However, under non-evaluative conditions, self-focused attention (manipulated by a mirror or audience presence) actually improved performance (copying text, Carver & Scheier, 1981). The explanation for this effect is that the presence of an audience in normal individuals creates a drive state in the performer, energizing the habits available to that person. Experiments conducted by Zajonc (1972, in: Innes & Young, 1975) demonstrated that self-focused attention speeds up performance, which does not necessarily mean a better performance. That is, in the mirror condition normal individuals were faster but not more accurate.

Taken together, the majority of studies in the area of social psychology suggest that the detrimental effects of self-focussing are specific for people who are somehow vulnerable, or specific under conditions that increase such vulnerability, such as evaluative conditions. Also, self-focused attention may well have beneficial effects on non-vulnerable individuals, under low evaluative conditions, and under conditions that require little attention (like copying text). Therefore, the role of self-focused attention, and its resulting dysfunctions, can be understood only by considering how it is likely to interact with other factors to produce a given disorder (Pyszczynski, Greenberg, Hamilton, & Nix, 1991). In the next section, we review the evidence for a relationship between self-focused attention and social anxiety. Firstly, we discuss the studies in which the tendency of being self-focused is investigated in relation to social anxiety and social phobia.

3.3. Trait self-focused attention

Trait self-focused attention refers to the tendency of a person to focus on certain aspects of the self during social situations. Self-focussing as a trait has typically been measured using the Self-Consciousness Scale (SCS, Fenigstein, Scheier, & Buss, 1975). It measures two types of self-consciousness, private self-consciousness and public self-consciousness. Private self-consciousness is defined as the general awareness of private aspects of the self, such as thoughts and feelings. However, it also includes items relating to self-reflective thinking such as “I am always trying to figure myself out”. Public self-consciousness is defined as the general awareness of the self as a social object that has an effect on others. In a well-designed experiment of Fenigstein (1979) participants who were classed as high and low in public self-consciousness participated in a conversation in which they were either accepted or rejected. High public self-conscious participants reported spending more time attending to themselves during the experiment, and the experience of rejection itself also made the participants engage in more self-attention. High public self-conscious participants were found to be more sensitive and react more negative to a rejection, even after controlling for private self-consciousness and social anxiety. An experiment of Reno and Kenny (1992) demonstrated that self-consciousness in unselected individuals does not necessarily have a negative impact on social interaction: female participants' public self-consciousness was positively related to the confederate's rating of the women's self-disclosure during an interaction.

Many studies have shown a correlation between public self-consciousness and social anxiety (Bögels et al., 1996, Fenigstein et al., 1975, Hope & Heimberg, 1988, Saboonchi & Lundh, 1997). The relationship between private self-consciousness and social anxiety is less clear, with some studies finding a correlation (Saboonchi & Lundh, 1997), and others finding no association (Bögels et al., 1996, Fenigstein et al., 1975). Hope and Heimberg (1988) recruited a population of patients with social phobia, and found that, after controlling for public self-consciousness, the (small) correlation between social anxiety and private self-consciousness disappeared, whereas the correlation between public self-consciousness and social anxiety remained after controlling for private self-consciousness. Thus, private and public self-consciousness are somewhat overlapping concepts, but it is public self-consciousness that is related to social anxiety and thus relevant for the present review. Furthermore, Hope & Heimberg (1988) demonstrated that within a clinical population of socially phobic patients, public self-consciousness was substantially correlated with anxiety, negative thinking, and performance during two social tasks (initiating a conversation and giving a speech). This result suggests that even among patients with social phobia, the amount of public self-consciousness still matters for the outcome of their social interactions.

Comparing clinical samples, social phobic patients display higher public self-consciousness levels than patients with other phobias and normal controls (Jostes et al., 1999, Saboonchi et al., 1999). The relationship between private self-consciousness and social anxiety in extreme groups is less clear, Saboonchi et al. (1999) found no differences between social phobia patients, patients with panic disorder, and normal controls on private self-consciousness, whereas Jostes et al. (1999) found that social phobic patients had elevated private self-consciousness levels compared to patients with other phobias and controls. (Saboonchi and Lundh, 1997) demonstrated that the relationship between public self-consciousness and social anxiety disappears after partialling out certain aspects of perfectionism: Concern over Mistakes, Doubts about Actions, and Socially Prescribed Perfectionism. This suggests that public self-consciousness is associated with social anxiety mainly in people who tend to be concerned over mistakes, to doubt their way of acting, and who perceive perfectionistic demands from their environment. Public self-consciousness in itself might be rather neutral with regard to social anxiety. (Carver, Peterson, Follansbee, & Schneier, 1983) demonstrated that level of test anxiety interacts with level of public self-consciousness. That is, participants who were low in test anxiety persisted longer on solving an insoluble problem than participants high in test anxiety, but only if they had also high levels of public self-consciousness, or, in other words, people high on test anxiety had lower persistence, but only if they also had high levels of public self-consciousness. Thus, this study shows that public self-consciousness can improve persistence (or decrease avoidance) in individuals who do not suffer from test anxiety, and have the opposite effect on individuals with test anxiety.

Bögels et al. (1996) investigated the relationship of several trait measures of attention on fear of blushing: public and private self-consciousness, and the tendency to focus attention one's arousal and to one's social performance. They found that only the tendency of the participants' to focus on their own arousal, and not their tendency to focus on their performance and their public and private self-consciousness, predicted fear of blushing in a regression analysis. This result emphasises that fear of arousal is not predicted by a general tendency to be aware of oneself publically or to self-focus, but by a specific tendency to be publically aware of or self-focus on one's physical arousal. This finding is in line with the conclusion of the review of Ingram (1990) that theoretical explication of self-focused attention requires not only reference to a particular process (internally directed attention) but also to a particular kind of content.

In conclusion, trait self-focused attention or public self-consciousness does not seem to have a negative effect on social interaction and performance, but through interacting with circumstances (like rejection) or vulnerabilities (like social anxiety) it can cause a socially anxious reaction. In the next paragraph we review evidence concerning a relationship between state self-focused attention and social anxiety.

3.4. State self-focused attention and social anxiety

State self-focused attention refers to on the spot focussing of attention onto aspects of the self. A number of studies have consistently and convincingly shown that high socially anxious, socially phobic, speech anxious, shy, and test anxious individuals, while in a social situation, display higher levels of self-reported self-focused attention than participants scoring low on these measures (Bögels & Lamers, 2002, Bögels et al., 2002, Daly et al., 1989, Hackmann et al., 1998, Melchior & Cheek, 1990, Perowne & Mansell, 2002), with only one exception (Stopa & Clark, 1993). However, socially anxious individuals have not reported lower states of other- or task-focused attention. For example, Mellings and Alden (2002) did not find a difference in self-reported task-focused attention comparing a large group socially-anxious students to controls, and Woody and Rodriguez (2000) did not find a difference between socially phobic patients and controls on task-focused attention. In addition, socially phobic patients recall social memories more often from an observer perspective compared to normal controls (Wells, Clark, & Ahmad, 1998) and compared to patients with other anxiety disorders (Wells & Papageorgiou, 1998a). This observer-perspective recall was specific for threatening social events, since socially phobic patients did not perform differently when recalling non-social non-threatening events. Even more convincing evidence for a relationship between social anxiety and self-focused attention came from a recent study of Mansell et al. (2003). Speech-anxious individuals directed their attention more to internal stimuli than to external stimuli when confronted with social threat (giving a speech). A correlational study of Daly et al. (1989) demonstrated further that high self-focused attention during a speech task was correlated with lower performance as well as lower self-evaluations. Mellings and Alden (2000) demonstrated that self-focused attention during a social interaction preceded negative biases in social judgements that were assessed the next day.

The causal relationship between self-focused attention and social anxiety has been investigated by experimentally heightening self- or task-focused attention in social situations and studying its effects on aspects of social anxiety. Experimental tasks used in such experiments typically consist of a social task like having a conversation or giving a speech. Attentional focus has been manipulated using a variety of techniques: seeing one's reflection in a mirror, instructions to self-focus, a video-camera, presence of a public, hearing one's own voice, talking or writing about oneself, heightening arousal or giving false arousal feedback, or by stories in which the hero becomes self-focused. Outcome measures have included: self-rated social anxiety, physiological measures, cognitive measures (e.g., attributions), performance measures (e.g., observer-rated social skills). Groups under study were analogue populations of individuals high or low on a range of measures related to social phobia: social anxiety, speech anxiety, dating anxiety, self-efficacy, self-esteem, test-anxiety, blushing anxiety, or social phobic patients compared to matched controls or patients with other types of anxiety disorders. We identified 12 relevant experiments, listed in Table 2. Four experiments were positive concerning a causal relationship between self-focused attention and aspects of social anxiety, one was negative, six produced partial evidence, that is, a causal effect was demonstrated for some variables but not for others, and one produced opposite effects, that is, self-focused attention facilitated performance for high test-anxious individuals. Concerning the specificity of the relationship between self-focused attention and social anxiety, six experiments found that the relationship between self-focused attention and social anxiety existed only in high socially anxious or otherwise vulnerable populations, two found a relationship irrespective of high or low social anxiety, one found an effect in the opposite direction: self-focused attention led to better performance if individuals were test anxious, and two experiments did not assess specificity.

3.5. Evidence for a causal role of self-focused attention

In the following section, we will critically discuss various aspects of the presented twelve experiments in order to answer the question of whether self-focused attention causes social anxiety and whether this effect is specific for people high in social anxiety.

Firstly, almost all experiments used numerous dependent variables, but in only one (Bögels et al., 2002) was a correction carried out for the risk of chance findings based on the number of tests carried out. As a result, many of the presented main effects for self-focused attention or interaction effects of attentional focus with social anxiety would not be significant after carrying out a Bonferonni correction. In addition, keeping in mind the fact that experiments with negative findings are more difficult to publish, and often are not even submitted for publication, the evidence for a causal relationship between focus of attention and social anxiety is not convincing.

Secondly, the way in which self-focused attention has been manipulated in most experiments is dubious given the problem under study: social anxiety. That is, socially anxious persons fear negative evaluation by other people. Therefore, manipulations in which a real public that will evaluate them (Alden, Teschuk, & Tee, 1992; Brockner & Hulton, 1978) or the presence of a video camera with the explicit or implicit suggestion that the recordings of the person's behaviour will be later evaluated (Alden et al., 1992, Panayiotou & Vrana, 1998) may simply enhance social anxiety rather than manipulate self-focused attention. The positive findings from the latter studies are therefore not convincing evidence for a causal role of self-focused attention. Such is true even if manipulation checks demonstrate that self-focused attention has been enhanced by the procedure, because the manipulation check has not been corrected for the anxiety enhancing effect of the manipulation (actually, the anxiety-enhancing effect is regarded as the dependent variable). Other manipulations have further problems. A mirror manipulation is an excellent self-focused attention manipulation in the context of non-social tasks (like performance on an IQ test), but in the context of a social task, it has the side effect of giving corrective feedback about the person's social skills, physical appearance, and presumed blushing, next to enhancing self-focused attention. This was demonstrated in the experiment of Bögels et al. (2002), in which low socially anxious individuals actually rated themselves as blushing more in the mirror condition than in the condition without a mirror, whereas no such effect was found for the high socially anxious individuals. Finally, though creative, the attention manipulation in the two experiments of Woody, 1996, Woody & Rodriguez, 2000 was also intrinsically anxiety enhancing. That is, in the other-focused condition the patient had to talk about the anxiety of another patient, whereas in the self-focused condition the socially phobic patient had to talk about his own anxiety in front of an audience. However, fear of being negatively evaluated by others because of one's own anxiety is actually the predominant fear of most socially phobic individuals. This leaves us at present with only one study in the area of social anxiety in which self-focused attention has been manipulated in a non-socially anxiety enhancing fashion; that of Bögels and Lamers (2002), which demonstrated that self-focused attention does enhance social anxiety, and that task-focused attention decreases social anxiety. However, it should be noted that this study also has limitations. It used a set of hypothetical scripts rather than a direct social situation. Therefore it lacks a degree of ecological validity and may be influenced by social desirability in the way that the scripts were presented.

Another concern with respect to the way attentional focus has been manipulated so far is that the majority of studies within the context of social anxiety did not manipulate attentional focus by focussing on the task (exceptions are the studies of Bögels & Lamers, 2002; Brockner et al., 1978). This is regrettable for several reasons. Much can be learned about the possible anxiety-decreasing and therefore therapeutic effects of task-focused attention on socially anxious individuals. Moreover, in the context of depression, two experiments conducted by Nix, Watson, Pyszczynski, and Greenberg (1995) demonstrated that depressed individuals who were instructed to task-focus exhibited lower levels of depression, while instructions to self-focus had no effect on the affective states of such subjects. Also, task-focus was found to reduce anxiety irrespective of subjects' initial level of depression. Next to the results of these experiments in the context of depression, there is another reason why focussing on the task is a potentially more powerful way to manipulate direction of attention in social anxiety. That is, the pre-existing higher self-focused attention levels of socially anxious individual in the reviewed experiments might overrule the self-focused attention enhancing effects of the experimental manipulation.

Another important aspect of the mentioned experiments is the amount of activity required for conducting the experiment. The two experiments of Woody, 1996, Woody & Rodriguez, 2000 demonstrated that self-focussing is only detrimental under passive conditions, i.e. listening to a speech. Many of the reviewed experiments require participants to become active (e.g. giving a speech, keeping the conversation going) and such activity might make participants automatically task-focused. Such an amount of activity might have little ecological validity for their daily life, in which they might avoid getting in such active social tasks. In fact, in daily life self-focused attention might result in subtle or active avoidance of the situation, which is in line with the findings of Alden et al. (1992) and Burgio, Merluzzi, and Pryor (1986) that self-focused attention resulted in avoidance.

3.6. Specificity of self-focused attention

Concerning the question of specificity of the detrimental effects of self-focused attention, or beneficial effects of task-focused attention, most of the experiments reviewed here demonstrated that the effect of attentional focus is specific to high socially-anxious individuals rather than low socially anxious individuals. This is consistent with the theory of Duval and Wicklund (1972) stating that self-focused attention has to interact with other variables like low outcome expectancy to have a detrimental effect. The only study in which no specificity was found concerned all three experiments by Bögels and Lamers (2002). Next to methodological explanations like a ceiling effect or the imaginary nature of the task, it might be that even low socially-anxious people have concerns about negative evaluations, and therefore, excessive self-focussing might be detrimental for anyone who is in a social situation that requires focussing on the task. Another specificity question is whether the detrimental effects of self-focussing are specific to social phobia or exist for other psychological disorders as well. Comparable detrimental effects of self-focused attention have been found for alcohol abusers and for depressed individuals (see Ingram, 1990). The co-morbidity between the three problems may explain this finding (Pyszczynski et al., 1991). For example, drinking reduces self-focused attention and social phobic patients often report drinking for this reason. Nevertheless, self-focused attention in the different disorders might take different forms. In depressed individuals, self-focused attention is typically used to refer to a state of “self-absorption”, worry, or rumination (Teasdale et al., 1995), in which they focus and dwell on negative thoughts about their own failures or low self-worth. These processes are assessed by certain items within the trait of private self-consciousness (Fenigstein et al., 1975). People with social phobia may also worry and ruminate both before and after feared social situations (Clark & Wells, 1995) and this may account for some studies finding that they have elated private self-consciousness. However, socially phobic individuals also appear to experience self-focused attention more in the presence of a real or imagined public, and their self-focused attention might have more the form of a sudden objective self-awareness, in which they see themselves through the eyes of the public. This form of self-focus has many similarities with the trait of public self-consciousness (Fenigstein et al., 1975), which we have shown to be elevated in social anxiety.

Another aspect of specificity concerns the role of gender. Self-focussing might have a different impact on males and females. The meta-analysis of Mor and Winquist (2002) demonstrated that self-focused attention was more strongly associated with negative affect in females than in males. A possible explanation for this finding is that males are more task-focused, whereas females have more attention for the way they are perceived by others. Indeed, females have higher ratings on public self-consciousness than males (Abrams, 1988).

4. Attention processes and treatment of social phobia

4.1. Evidence for an association between change in attention and in social phobia

From the previous sections it can be concluded that certain attention processes are strongly associated with social phobia, although the direction of the association is not fully clear, that is, hypervigilance, attention away from social cues, and self-focused attention may cause social fears or may result from (be a by-product of) social fears. Based on the association between the reviewed attention processes and social anxiety, it can be expected that (I) attention processes change as a result of effective treatment of social phobia, irrespective of the type of treatment; and (II) attention retraining in social phobic patients will reduce their social fears. If attention processes cause social anxiety, one would predict that (III) change in attention processes predicts and precedes decrease in social fears. In the following section, we will review evidence for the three predictions, and conclude with a discussion of possible working mechanisms of attention retraining in social phobia, in which we will integrate insights from both hypervigilance and self-focused attention theory and research.

Research on the relationship between treatment and reaction-time measures of attentional bias has been restricted to the emotional Stroop test. Mattia et al. (1993) and Lundh and Öst (2001) found that the emotional Stroop effect diminished after successful cognitive behavioural therapy, indicating that the bias is a feature of the disorder rather than a trait-like vulnerability factor. These findings support prediction I, but they are qualified by the ambiguities concerning the emotional Stroop test (see earlier).

In line with prediction I, that attention processes will change after social phobia treatment, several studies found that trait and state self-focused attention and private and public self-consciousness reduce after successful treatment of social phobia (Bögels, 1994; Bögels et al., 1997, Hofmann, 2000, Mulkens et al., 2001, Woody et al., 1997). However, self-reported trait and state task-focused attention does not increase after successful treatment (Hofmann, 2000, Woody et al., 1997). Since social phobic patients were not found to have different levels of task-focused attention than normal controls (see Trait Self-focused Attention), the latter finding is not surprising. Hofmann (2000) furthermore found that reduction of attention focus to negative aspects of the self correlated significantly with change in social phobia.

Consistent with prediction II, that attention re-training will reduce social anxiety, Bögels et al. (1997) found that a new attention training called task concentration, in which patients are taught to reduce self-focused attention by focusing their full attention on their task and environment, led to large reductions in fear of blushing and social phobia in two patients with erytrophobia. At the same time, Wells, White, and Carter (1997) described positive effects of attention training, consisting of focussing attention on sounds, on a patient with social phobia. Attention training that consisted of increasing attention to task-relevant variables was as effective as cognitive restructuring for students (n=25) with test anxiety (Wise & Haynes, 1983). Task concentration was more effective than applied relaxation in a treatment outcome study for socially phobic patients (n=65) with fear of blushing, trembling or sweating (Bögels, 2004). Moreover, several studies found that teaching social phobic patients to focus attention outward enhances the effects of cognitive and/or behavioural treatment of social phobia. Wells and Papageorgiou (1998b) demonstrated that exposure plus focusing attention outward indeed led to a shift from observer to field perspective and more effectively reduced social anxiety than exposure alone, in a series of case studies (n=8). They proposed that directing attention externally promotes the processing of information that may contradict dysfunctional beliefs, such as “I am making a fool of myself” (see Clark & Wells, 1995). Mulkens et al. (2001) found exposure plus task concentration training to be more effective in reducing blushing phobic patients' dysfunctional beliefs than exposure alone, in a larger treatment outcome study (n=26). Bögels (2004) found that task concentration plus cognitive therapy, compared to applied relaxation plus cognitive therapy, led to greater long-term reduction in fear of blushing, trembling and sweating and in dysfunctional beliefs in patients with fear of showing somatic symptoms as the primary complaint.

Mindfulness training is a form of meditation training in which people are taught to focus their attention first to their bodies, then to their breathing, and finally to the here and now. In a series of case studies (n=10), a combination of mindfulness-plus task concentration training of only nine sessions was effective for patients with generalised social phobia (Bögels, Sijbers & Voncken, 2004). Attention- and task concentration training differ from mindfulness training in their attention focus: attention training promotes external attention, whereas mindfulness training promotes external as well as internal attention (to the body, breath). One could reason that mindfulness training might heighten self-focused attention, and, therefore, have adverse effects. However, mindfulness instructs patients to become aware of their automatic tendency to self-focus or ruminate, and to regain control over attention processes, thereby allowing individuals to disengage from being self-focused.

The first evidence for prediction III, that change in attention processes predicts and precedes change in social phobia, comes from the treatment outcome study of Bögels (2004). Reduction in self-focused attention early in treatment was a significant predictor of long-term change in social phobia and fear of blushing, trembling and sweating and predicted further reduction in negative beliefs about blushing, trembling and sweating.

Taken together, these findings suggest that successful cognitive-behavioural treatment for social phobia changes attention processes, and treatment procedures that directly influence attention in social phobia appear to be effective and seem to enhance the effects of cognitive-behavioural therapy. The evidence reviewed in this section also provides indirect support for a causal role of attention processes in social anxiety. However, further controlled studies are required, for example to test Prediction III.

4.2. Change mechanisms in attention training

In the last section we will discuss the working mechanisms involved in the effectiveness of attention training and possibly in other cognitive therapies that involve the manipulation of attention (e.g. Clark & Ehlers, 1993). Of course, it is likely that part of the mechanism of attention training is through non-specific factors of therapy. Below, we will concentrate on the likely attention processes at work. Six possible mechanisms are described below. Importantly, they are overlapping mechanisms that may not necessarily be mutually exclusive. For an alternative account, see Wells (2000).

4.2.1. Reducing hypervigilance

Although the reviewed attention procedures are somewhat different in their focus, what they have in common is that patients learn to redirect their attention to the wider aspects of their social environment and to broader aspects of the self, rather than focusing in on feared aspects of their self (e.g. hot cheeks) and their social environment (e.g. a frown). States of high emotionality, arousal and anxiety all reduce the breadth of attention and the range of cues that is used Eysenck (1992)). Thereby, the first change mechanism might be countering social phobic patients' hypervigilance to social threat. That is, paying attention to non-threatening aspects of the social environment and the self will help re-appraise the situation and improve proficiency. At present we do not know whether hypervigilance is better reduced by directing attention to multiple elements of the internal and external environment, or whether focusing attention on a single non-threatening stimulus would be sufficient.

4.2.2. Reducing avoidance

The second, related path to change concerns the countering of social phobic patients' attention away from perceived threatening internal or external social cues. By continuing to pay attention towards perceived threat rather than switching to avoid the threat, social threat can be re-appraised, and proficiency improved. It may appear somewhat contradictory to recommend both focusing attention on non-threatening elements of the environment (see 4.2.1.) while also recommending reducing avoidance of threat stimuli. However they are not necessarily contradictory. One key similarity may be the motivation behind the redirection of attention. Hypervigilance and attentional avoidance may both be motivated by the desire to identify potential dangers and then avoid them. In contrast, broadening attention to non-threatening elements of the environment is carried out in therapy to help reappraise the perception of danger rather than to escape it. Reducing attentional avoidance in therapy is also aimed at challenging the individual's perception of danger through blocking their attempts to escape and seek safety; it does not involve enhancing their vigilance to detect further threats. Instead, the patient is encouraged to sustain their attention on the existing threat and reappraise it in the wider, non-threatening, context. Interestingly, it is possible that both methods of treatment may require controlled and sustained attention on the potential threat and non-threat stimuli to reach an accurate evaluation of danger before a response is elicited (see below). In contrast, the evidence suggests that the dysfunctional processes of hypervigilance and avoidance appear to be relatively fast and automatic processes.

4.2.3. Reducing self-focused attention

By focussing attention outward, patients become subject rather than object in a social situation (or actor rather than spectator, or perceive from a field-perspective rather than observer-perspective), which leads to a qualitatively different way of processing information, for example, to external attributions of failure.

4.2.4. Mindfulness

The fourth path to change concerns the countering of “mindless” information processing or rumination. Through awareness of how the (ruminating) mind works, and focussing on the here and now, “mindful experiencing” is started, in which new information can be connected to implicational (higher-order) meaning structures (Teasdale et al., 1995).

4.2.5. Control

The fifth path to change through attention training concerns a sense of control over attention processes. A common goal of almost all psychotherapies is to enhance patients' sense of control (Shapiro, Barkham, Rees, Hardy, & et al., 1994). Attention training increases the sense of control over attention processes. As suggested above, improved control over attention may allow the individual to sustain attention on internal and external stimuli in order to allow the reappraisal of danger before they elicit a response. In addition, the evidence reviewed earlier suggested both that attention is captured automatically by threatening stimuli (e.g. Mogg & Bradley, 2002), and that people with social phobia may also have difficulties disengaging their attention from threatening words (Amir et al., 2003). If patients can improve their control over their attention they may be more able to disengage when their attention is automatically captured by threatening stimuli.

4.2.6. Self-esteem

Finally, lack of concentration itself might confirm schemas of worthlessness and ineffectiveness, not only in depressed patients but also in patients suffering from anxiety disorders. In contrast, enhanced concentration (also called “flow”) increases self-esteem and feelings of happiness.

5. Conclusions

We first reviewed the evidence for the role of attention processes in social anxiety and social phobia. Across the wide range of experimental studies, there is reasonable evidence for a brief vigilance and prolonged avoidance of social threats in paradigms that are high in ecological validity. However, further studies are required to explore the contextual factors that determine which bias is observed in which contexts, especially within clinical populations. The evidence also suggests that self-focused attention is elevated in social anxiety and social phobia, and that it is relatively specific to public features of the self.

Second, we reviewed the evidence for a causal role of attention processes in social phobia. The causal role for vigilance and avoidance has not been adequately assessed and requires investigation. The causal role of self-focused attention has been researched in more detail, suggesting that it may increase social anxiety, but the studies suffer from important methodological limitations.

Third, we investigated whether the above findings were specific to social anxiety. In support of specificity, the findings of vigilance and avoidance of social threat have not been observed in low socially anxious individuals, and the effects tend to remain when other factors such as trait anxiety and dysphoria have been assessed. Similarly, the negative effects of inducing self-focused attention also tend to be absent, less severe or even beneficial in low socially anxious individuals. Nevertheless, it is important to note that the attention processes reviewed here and their negative effects have been observed in other disorders such as alcoholism, other anxiety disorders, depression and schizophrenia (for a comprehensive review, see Harvey et al., 2004). Future research needs to explore whether the shared effect is due to the high levels of social anxiety in these populations, or whether a separate factor is responsible (e.g. attention control).

Fourth, we asked how attention processes are related to change in social anxiety, especially during treatment in social phobia. There is persuasive evidence that attention processes are indeed related to change in social phobia and may mediate improvement in symptoms during treatment. This evidence is encouraging for treatments that target attention processes in their protocol. Nevertheless, further research needs to assess attention processes using more tightly controlled paradigms and explore whether it predicts change over longer periods. We have also proposed six possible mechanisms of change that can be studied in future investigations.

Finally, it is important to note that although the vigilance-avoidance and self-focused attention accounts were reviewed separately, they are likely to be related. Threat stimuli could include internal cues such as anxiety symptoms, and therefore vigilance to them is likely to trigger self-focused attention. Furthermore, avoidance of external social threat may entail that attention becomes focused on the self instead. Further research needs to explore the relationship between these two accounts and may lead to the development of a position that accommodates the evidence for each of them.
